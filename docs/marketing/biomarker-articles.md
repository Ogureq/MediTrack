## 1. LDL Cholesterol

**SEO title:** LDL Cholesterol Explained: What a High or Low Number Means

**Meta description:** A plain-language guide to LDL cholesterol — what the test measures, what high and low results typically indicate, and questions worth asking your doctor.

### What LDL cholesterol actually is

LDL stands for low-density lipoprotein — one of the particles that carries
cholesterol through your bloodstream. It's often nicknamed "bad
cholesterol," which isn't wrong, but it's a simplification. Cholesterol
itself isn't harmful; your body needs it to build cell membranes and make
certain hormones. The concern with LDL specifically is what happens when
there's more of it circulating than your body is using: it can contribute
to plaque buildup along artery walls over time, which is why it gets so
much attention on a standard lipid panel.

LDL is usually reported in mg/dL. A commonly used reference point is under
100 mg/dL, though what counts as an appropriate target for any individual
depends on other risk factors — family history, blood pressure, whether
you smoke, other lipid values on the same panel — that a single number
can't capture on its own.

### What a high result typically means

A higher LDL result usually reflects some combination of diet, activity
level, genetics, and other health conditions — it's rarely just one thing.
Elevated LDL, especially over years, is one of several factors clinicians
weigh when discussing cardiovascular risk. It's not, by itself, a diagnosis
of anything, and a single elevated draw is not the same as a sustained
pattern — which is part of why looking at LDL over multiple draws tends to
be more informative than reacting to any one result.

### What a low result typically means

A lower LDL result is generally considered desirable and is often
associated with lower cardiovascular risk. Very low results are uncommon
and not typically a concern on their own, though any result that seems out
of step with your history is worth mentioning at your next appointment
simply so it's on record.

### Questions worth asking your doctor

- Given my other risk factors, what LDL range makes sense as a target for me specifically?
- Does this result change anything about diet, activity, or medication, or is it worth watching over another draw or two first?
- How does my LDL relate to my HDL and total cholesterol on this same panel?
- How often should I retest, given this result and my history?

### Tracking it over time

A single LDL number is a snapshot; the more useful picture is usually the
trend across several draws — and knowing when you're actually due for the
next one. Gemocode logs each draw for free and shows your own LDL history as

## 2. TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone)

**SEO title:** TSH Explained: What Your Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone Level Means

**Meta description:** What TSH measures, what a high or low result commonly indicates, and the questions to bring to your next doctor's visit.

### What TSH actually is

TSH — thyroid-stimulating hormone — isn't made by your thyroid at all;
it's made by your pituitary gland, and it's the signal your pituitary
sends to tell your thyroid how much hormone to produce. That's why TSH is
usually the first test ordered when thyroid function is in question: it's
an indirect but sensitive gauge of how hard your body is having to "ask"
your thyroid to work. A commonly used adult reference range is roughly 0.4
to 4.0 mIU/L, though labs vary slightly and your own baseline matters more
than a single cutoff.

### What a high result typically means

Because TSH is a signal, a higher-than-usual result often means your
pituitary is working harder to prompt hormone production — commonly
associated with an underactive thyroid. It's rarely interpreted alone;
clinicians typically look at it alongside free T4 (and sometimes free T3)
to understand the fuller picture, since TSH by itself doesn't say why it's
elevated. A mildly elevated result on one draw isn't automatically the
same story as a sustained elevation across several draws — which is part
of why clinicians often ask for a repeat test before drawing conclusions,
rather than acting on a single number.

### What a low result typically means

A lower-than-usual TSH can suggest the opposite dynamic — the pituitary is
signaling less because thyroid hormone levels are already higher than
needed, often associated with an overactive thyroid. Certain medications
can also affect TSH independent of thyroid function itself, which is one
reason a low result is worth discussing in context rather than reacting to
in isolation. Pregnancy and some pituitary conditions can also shift TSH
outside its usual range for reasons unrelated to the thyroid itself, which
is another reason this test tends to be read alongside a person's broader
history rather than as a number that means the same thing for everyone.

### Questions worth asking your doctor

- Should this be interpreted alongside free T4 or free T3, and were those tested too?
- Is this a one-time result or part of a pattern across my recent draws?
- Are any of my current medications known to affect TSH?
- What symptoms, if any, should I watch for between now and my next test?

### Tracking it over time

TSH tends to be more informative as a trend than as a single value,
especially for anyone managing an ongoing thyroid condition, since the
direction a result is moving often matters as much as where it sits on a
given day — and as when you're actually due to check it again. Gemocode

## 3. HbA1c (Hemoglobin A1c)

**SEO title:** HbA1c Explained: What Your A1C Result Means

**Meta description:** A plain-language breakdown of HbA1c — what the test measures, what high and low results typically indicate, and questions to bring to your doctor.

### What HbA1c actually is

HbA1c — often just called A1C — estimates your average blood sugar over
roughly the past two to three months. Unlike a fasting glucose test, which
captures a single moment, A1C reflects a longer window because it measures
the percentage of hemoglobin (the oxygen-carrying protein in red blood
cells) that has glucose attached to it. Red blood cells live for about
three months, which is why the test has that particular lookback. It's
reported as a percentage, and a commonly used reference range for a
non-diabetic adult is roughly 4.0% to 5.6%.

### What a higher result typically means

A higher A1C reflects a higher average blood sugar over the preceding
months rather than any single meal or day. Results somewhat above the
typical range are often discussed in terms of prediabetes risk, and
higher results still are discussed in the context of diabetes management —
but where any specific number falls on that spectrum, and what it means
for a given person, is a conversation for a clinician who has the full
picture, not something a single percentage settles on its own. Because the
test reflects a rolling average, a result that seems high after one
unusually stressful or unwell stretch isn't automatically a long-term
pattern — which is another reason a repeat test, rather than a single
reaction, is the typical next step.

### What a lower result typically means

A lower A1C is uncommon as a concern by itself. On the rare occasions a
low result stands out, it can sometimes relate to conditions affecting
red blood cell lifespan (like certain forms of anemia), since the test
depends on those cells living a typical lifespan. Any unusual result —
high or low — relative to your own history is worth flagging.

### Questions worth asking your doctor

- How does this A1C compare to my previous results, and is there a trend?
- Does this result suggest any change to diet, activity, or medication, or is it a "watch and retest" situation?
- Were any conditions present (recent illness, certain blood disorders) that could affect how accurately A1C reflects my average blood sugar right now?
- How often should I retest given this result?

### Tracking it over time

Because A1C is inherently a trailing average, seeing it plotted across
multiple draws tends to be more useful than any single result on its own —
a slow upward drift across a year of draws tells a different story than
one high result surrounded by otherwise-typical ones, even if the most
recent number looks identical either way. Gemocode adds each draw to your

## 4. Vitamin D

**SEO title:** Vitamin D Levels Explained: What High and Low Results Mean

**Meta description:** What a Vitamin D (25-hydroxy) blood test measures, what high and low results commonly indicate, and questions worth asking your doctor.

### What the Vitamin D test actually is

The Vitamin D blood test — technically 25-hydroxyvitamin D, or "25-OH
Vitamin D" on many lab forms — measures the storage form of vitamin D
circulating in your blood. Vitamin D plays a role in calcium regulation
and bone health, among other functions, and your level reflects a mix of
sun exposure, diet, supplementation, and how your body processes it. It's
reported in ng/mL, and a commonly used reference range for adequacy is
roughly 30 to 100 ng/mL, though labs and guidelines vary somewhat on where
"adequate" starts.

### What a low result typically means

Low Vitamin D is common, especially in people with limited sun exposure,
certain diets, darker skin (which produces vitamin D from sunlight less
efficiently), or who live at higher latitudes with less year-round sun.
A low result on its own doesn't diagnose a specific condition, but
persistently low levels are a common reason clinicians discuss
supplementation, dietary changes, or safe sun-exposure guidance — the
right response depends on how low, how consistent, and what else is going
on for that person. Results also tend to run lower in winter months in
many parts of the world simply because of reduced sun exposure, which is
one reason a single off-season result is often interpreted alongside the
time of year it was drawn rather than in isolation.

### What a high result typically means

High Vitamin D is less common and is usually related to supplementation
(it's harder to get an excessive amount from sun or diet alone). Because
vitamin D is fat-soluble and stored in the body rather than excreted
quickly, taking high-dose supplements without guidance is one of the more
common ways this happens. Elevated results are generally reviewed
alongside how much supplementation someone is taking, since dosage
adjustment is often the most relevant piece of context.

### Questions worth asking your doctor

- Is my current level considered adequate for my situation, and what's a reasonable target?
- If it's low, does that call for supplementation, dietary change, sun exposure, or just a retest in a few months?
- If I'm already supplementing, is my current dose appropriate given this result?
- How often is it worth retesting Vitamin D, given typical seasonal variation?

### Tracking it over time

Vitamin D levels can shift with the seasons, supplementation habits, and
time spent outdoors, so a single result is often less useful than seeing
it across a year or more — a low reading in February and a normal one in
August can both be unremarkable for the same person once the seasonal
